# Brand Voice and Style Guide

Canonical reference for Triptease writing style. Built from 65 blog posts (2024-2026), 12 landing pages, 40+ HubSpot emails, and the internal Tone of Voice Guide. This document is the system prompt foundation for [[Triptease Studio]]'s Style Layer.

---

## 1. Brand voice: the non-negotiables

**One-liner:** Triptease is the Data Marketing Platform for hotels. Integrated full-funnel marketing for more direct bookings.

**Five words that define the voice: direct, confident, practical, pro-hotelier, human.**

Triptease writes like a sharp, data-fluent colleague who is always on the side of the hotelier. Not a vendor. Not a consultant. A trusted insider who knows the industry cold, gets straight to the point, and is honest even when the truth is uncomfortable.

**What the voice is:**
- Smart but never self-satisfied
- Expert but never patronizing
- Direct: no hedging, no preamble, get to the point
- Confident: state things plainly, back them with data
- Slightly provocative: challenge assumptions, name uncomfortable truths
- Practical: always end with something actionable
- Warm but not soft: cares about hoteliers, but doesn't coddle them
- Occasionally wry: light humor works when it's natural and self-aware ("just ask Classik Hotels", "about the time it takes to make a good cup of coffee")
- Honest about limitations: "We'll admit it, in the past, optimizing Messages for mobile could have been easier"

**What the voice is never:**
- Salesy or pushy
- Vague ("solutions that work for your business")
- Fluffy ("we're passionate about helping hotels thrive")
- Academic or overly formal
- Neutral on the OTA question
- Hypey or gimmicky
- Buzzword-heavy

## 2. Tone by content type

**Blog posts:** Authoritative and analytical. Lead with urgency or a news hook. Build the case with data. Land with practical takeaways. Soft product mention at the end. Close with forward momentum, not a hard sell.

**Customer stories / case studies:** Factual and results-led. The hotel's problem comes first, then the Triptease solution, then the numbers. Metrics are the hero. No fluff.

**Product landing pages:** Punchy and benefit-first. Short sentences. Every line earns its place. Role-specific framing ([[Revenue Manager]] / [[Marketing Manager]] / [[E-Commerce Manager]]). CTAs are action verbs: "Get more revenue direct", "Create personalized marketing", "Increase website conversions."

**Product announcements / news stories:** Clear value proposition headline. Founder quote. Specific feature descriptions framed as problems solved. Accessible to non-technical readers.

**Direct Booking Digest (newsletter):** More conversational than blog posts. Shorter paragraphs. "Here's what happened / here's why it matters to you" structure per item. Named sections: "The Unmissables" (three deep-dive items) + "Quick reads." Intro sets up the whole issue. Bold key phrases within paragraphs.

**Weekly CEC emails (customer story promotion):** Short and data-led. One bold italic opening hook. One hotel name + two to three metric bullets. One sentence of context. One CTA link. Sign off. Total body: 100-150 words.

**Product announcement emails:** Hook with the problem. Name the product in bold. Benefits as short paragraphs. CTA to the resource. Warmer tone than landing pages.

**Re-engagement / reactivation emails:** Disarmingly honest. Acknowledge the situation directly. Lead with something new and valuable. Two or three CTA choices (one to engage, one to exit). No pressure.

**Event emails (ITB, DBS):** "We were there, here's what mattered" framing. Use first person plural. Lead with the most surprising or counterintuitive takeaway in the subject line.

## 3. Sentence structure

Mix short punchy sentences with longer analytical ones. Short sentences land the key point. Longer sentences build context.

**Do this:**
- "The shift is already happening."
- "We wouldn't hold our breath."
- "A little can go a long way."
- "Traditional SEO just isn't enough anymore."
- "The recovery is real. But it isn't equal."
- "Hotel marketing is facing a seismic shift."
- "2,500 rooms. Sky-high OTA cancellation rates. Rock-bottom ADRs."
- "This idea that Booking.com's offering is 'optional' to hotels is debatable."

**Don't do this:**
- "In this blog post, we will explore the ways in which..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "Hotels may want to consider potentially..."
- Starting any sentence with "Leverage", "Synergize", or "Streamline"

**Open with the point, not the setup.** The first sentence of every piece should contain the most important thing. Readers scan: earn their attention immediately.

**Use rhetorical questions as section headers** to signal provocation and frame what follows:
- "Will Booking.com actually change?"
- "What does this mean for hotels?"
- "So: is Travel Tuesday the new Black Friday?"

**Address the reader directly.** Use "you", "your hotel", "hoteliers": not "properties" or "the hotel" (third-person distance).

**Features always land with a "why it matters" line.** Never just describe what something does. Always follow with the implication for the reader's hotel.

**Close with forward momentum, not a hard sell.** Even product emails end by pointing toward what comes next rather than just pushing a demo.

## 4. Article and content structure

### Standard blog post
1. **Hook**: a news event, data point, or industry shift that makes this urgent right now
2. **Context**: why it matters, what's at stake for hotels
3. **Analysis**: what Triptease (or cited experts) make of it
4. **Practical section**: numbered takeaways titled "What hotels should do" or "Key takeaway for hotels:"
5. **Soft CTA**: link to demo, deeper resource, or "get in touch"

### Customer story / case study
1. The hotel's challenge (named hotel, specific pain point)
2. The Triptease solution (concise: one paragraph)
3. The results (metrics first: always lead with the number)
4. Brief "why this matters" for other hoteliers

### Product landing page
1. Punchy headline (benefit, not feature name)
2. One-line value prop per audience role
3. Feature descriptions as outcomes ("Beat OTA prices automatically")
4. Social proof or data point
5. CTA

### Product announcement
1. Headline: what changed and why it matters
2. Founder or CPO quote
3. Feature-by-feature descriptions (brief paragraphs, not bullets)
4. How to access

### Direct Booking Digest
- Numbered issue + date stamp
- One bold italic hook line
- Intro paragraph: what's in this issue and why it matters now
- "The Unmissables": three items, each with source attribution, two to three paragraphs of analysis, and a "what this means for hotels" implication
- "Quick reads": three to five shorter items, one paragraph each, CTA link
- No hard close: issue ends with the last quick read

### Weekly CEC email (customer story)
- Subject line: lead with the metric or outcome, not the hotel name
- Preview text: one data point or result that adds to the subject
- Greeting: "Hi there,"
- Opening line: one bold italic hook statement
- Context: two to three sentences on the hotel and the challenge
- Results: two to three bullet points with specific metrics (using the star symbol)
- Transition: "How did they get there?"
- CTA: "Read the full [Hotel Name] story"
- Sign-off: "Best regards, / The Triptease Team"

### Re-engagement email
- Subject line: honest, slightly disarming ("Should we stop emailing you?")
- Opening: acknowledge you've been sending for a while, no hard feelings if they've moved on
- Value: two to three sentences on what's new and worth knowing
- CTA options: one to re-engage, one to unsubscribe
- Sign-off: "Best, / The Triptease Team"

## 6. Words and phrases to avoid

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Leverage | Use |
| Synergies | (just say what the thing does) |
| Streamline | Speed up / simplify |
| Seamless | (just describe the experience) |
| Solutions | (name the specific product or capability) |
| Best-in-class | (cite data instead) |
| Revolutionary | (cite the change instead) |
| Game-changing | (only if evidence supports it, then show it) |
| Empower | Help / enable |
| Holistic | (just describe the scope) |
| Robust | (describe what it actually does) |
| Properties | Hotels |
| Accommodation | Hotels |
| Cutting-edge | (describe the actual capability) |
| Next-gen | (describe what's new) |
| Em dashes | Use commas, colons, or a new sentence |
| Disruptive | (show the disruption with data) |
| "Leaking money" / "leaking revenue" | Use specific, factual language: "losing bookings to OTAs", "OTA commissions eating into margin", "direct revenue left on the table" |

## 7. Hard rules: confirmed March 2026

### US English: non-negotiable
Personalize, optimize, analyze, travelers, behavior, color, center. Not personalise, optimise, travellers, behaviour, colour, centre. Some older published content uses British English: that is a legacy issue. All new content generated by [[Triptease Studio]] uses US English.

### Em dashes: prohibited. Non-negotiable.
No em dashes anywhere: blog, email, social, landing page. Replace with a comma, a colon, or a new sentence. [[Triptease Studio]] will never output an em dash. Any draft containing one should be treated as an error.

### No exclamation marks
Except in very specific high-energy contexts (product launches, event announcements). Even then, use sparingly: maximum one per piece.

### No passive voice
Where active is possible: "Hotels see a 2.5x increase" not "A 2.5x increase is seen by hotels."

### Sentence case headlines
"All talk, or reclaiming control?" not "All Talk, Or Reclaiming Control?"

## 8. How to present data

**Lead with the number, then contextualize:**
- Good: "Undercut rate reduced from 50-70% to less than 5%."
- Bad: "McDreams saw a significant reduction in their undercut rate."

**Compare to a benchmark or prior state:**
- "Price Match CTR of 20%: double what McDreams typically sees on metasearch"
- "2.5x increase in CTR when the direct price is the cheapest"
- "Search volumes grew more than fivefold from 2021 to 2023"
- "Rio Las Vegas grew metasearch revenue 71% in six months"
- "Evans Hotels recaptured $1.3M in the first half of 2025 at a 54x return"

**Always attribute external research:**
- "According to McKinsey..."
- "Skift's keynote revealed..."
- "Google reports that..."
- "SEMRush analysis shows..."
- "Triptease data gathered from 10,000+ properties shows..."
- "Studies show 40% of consumers now use LLMs for travel search"

**Triptease's own data is cited as authoritative.** Phrases like "Triptease data shows", "our global hotel data indicates", and "based on data from 10,000+ properties" signal credibility without boasting.

**Specific numbers always beat vague claims.** "60% of the time" beats "frequently." "12.1x ROAS" beats "strong returns."

**Numbers rule:** Spell out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above. Always use the % symbol.

  - Charlie Osmond, Co-founder
  - Alasdair Snow, Co-founder and Chief Product Officer

## 10. How Triptease talks about OTAs

Critical but factual. Named directly. Never vague.

- **Booking.com** named most frequently, most critically
- Practices described plainly: "price undercutting", "commission fees", "parity clauses", "anti-competitive behaviour", "outsized influence", "market dominance", "junk fees"
- The hotel-OTA relationship is always framed as a power imbalance that hotels can and should address
- "OTA dependency" is the core problem; "direct bookings" and "direct channel" are the solution
- Regulatory news (DMA, ECJ rulings, FTC junk fee rules) covered factually with Triptease's view clearly stated at the end
- Skeptical but not hyperbolic: "We wouldn't hold our breath": acknowledges that change is slow

## 11. Email-specific rules

### Subject lines
Subject lines are the single most important line of copy. They are punchy, specific, and often provocative. They never describe the email: they earn the open.

**Formats that work:**
- Statement that creates urgency: "The direct booking gap is widening. Close it."
- Contrarian take: "The hotels investing in AI right now might be making a mistake"
- Metric-led: "GCP Hospitality's pragmatic path to direct growth"
- Question that challenges: "Should we stop emailing you?"
- News hook + implication: "The US hotel market just split in two + RevPAR is back (but not for everyone)"
- Ellipsis for intrigue: "The end of SEO... as we know it?"

**Rules:**
- Under 50 characters where possible
- Sentence case
- No exclamation marks (unless product launch)
- Never start with "Introducing" unless it's a major product launch

### Preview text
The preview text complements, never repeats, the subject. It adds the metric or hook the subject teased.

- Subject: "GCP Hospitality's pragmatic path to direct growth" / Preview: "18x metasearch ROAS and $170k+ from exit intent. Here's how."
- Subject: "The hotels investing in AI right now might be making a mistake" / Preview: "Our top ITB Berlin takeaways, including one that surprised the whole room."
- Subject: "The US hotel market just split in two..." / Preview: "Luxury is pulling ahead, AI crawlers are reshaping search, and Rio Las Vegas just posted 17x ROAS with Triptease."

### Greeting and sign-off
- Greeting: "Hi there," (consistent across all campaigns)
- Personalized greeting (when data is available): "Hi {{custom.firstName}},"
- Sign-off options:
  - "Best regards, / The Triptease Team" (standard)
  - "Best, / The Triptease Team" (more casual, re-engagement)
  - "The Triptease Team" (digest, no preceding farewell)

### Email length
- **Weekly CEC (customer story):** 100-175 words body
- **Product announcement:** 200-300 words body
- **Direct Booking Digest:** 350-500 words total across all sections
- **Re-engagement:** 100-150 words body
- **Event email:** 200-300 words body

### CTA style in emails
Use directional arrow links or bold text links: not buttons for in-body CTAs.

**CTAs must be outcome-specific, not generic.** They describe what the reader will see or gain, not the content type.

- Good: "See how Rio Las Vegas grew metasearch revenue 71%"
- Good: "See how Song Hotel went from 7% to 11% direct share"
- Good: "See the five trends reshaping hotel marketing in 2026"
- Bad: "Read the blog"
- Bad: "Read the full story" (too generic: specify the outcome)
- Bad: "Click here to learn more"

Reactivation-specific:
- "Worth a conversation: Book a call / I've moved on: Unsubscribe"

## 12. CTAs: how Triptease closes content

Always soft. Offer help, never pressure.

**Standard CTAs used:**
- "Get in touch with our direct booking experts"
- "Request a demo" / "Book a demo"
- "Watch the full webinar" / "Watch the on-demand webinar here"
- "Download the free guide"
- "See what Triptease could do for your hotel"

**CTA rule: always outcome-specific.** The link text describes what the reader will discover or gain, not what they will do. "See how [Hotel] achieved [result]" beats "Read the case study." "Watch how hotels are using [product] to [outcome]" beats "Watch the webinar." Generic verbs (read, click, learn more, find out) are only acceptable when the outcome is already clear from context.

**Rules:**
- CTAs go at the end of the article or as a "if you want to go deeper" suggestion inline
- Never a mid-article hard sell
- Product links are embedded naturally in the text when referencing a relevant capability
- Product blogs end with clear CTAs; thought leadership and digest content ends more softly

## 13. Formatting rules

- **Headlines:** Sentence case: "All talk, or reclaiming control?" not "All Talk, Or Reclaiming Control?"
- **Subheadings:** Numbered for listicles, H2 for standard long-form articles. Frame as a benefit or insight, not a label: "Say goodbye to random pop-ups" not "Pop-up management"
- **Bold:** Key terms, product names, pull-out stats, section labels ("Key takeaway for hotels:"). Frequent within paragraphs in email copy.
- **Italic:** Used sparingly for publication names (*The Direct Booking Digest*), emphasis on a specific word, or the opening hook line in CEC emails
- **Lists:** Used for practical tips, feature descriptions, numbered takeaways, metric results. Not for analysis or conclusions.
- **No em dashes** in body copy (brand rule: non-negotiable)
- **Numbers:** One through nine in words. 10 and above in numerals. Always use the % symbol.
- **English:** US English: personalize, optimize, analyze, travelers, behavior. Non-negotiable.
- **Dates:** Day Month Year format in editorial ("27 March 2026"); "No. 23 | March 3, 2026" format for digest issues
- **Article length:** Blog posts 600-1,500 words. Guides 1,500-3,000. Case studies 400-700. Quick tips 300-600.
- **LinkedIn posts:** 150-300 words. Hook in first line (before "more" cut). Three to five hashtags max.

## 14. Persona-specific writing rules

### Revenue Manager content
- Lead with financial outcomes: RevPAR, ROAS, direct revenue, commission savings
- Use rate strategy vocabulary naturally: parity, undercutting, CPA, CPC, bid optimization
- Practical and numbers-first: this persona scans for data
- Preferred proof: benchmarks, percentage improvements, time savings

### Marketing Manager content
- Lead with channel performance and campaign outcomes
- Use marketing vocabulary: retargeting, paid search, metasearch, conversion rate, CTR
- Show how Triptease connects channels ("no more juggling multiple systems")
- Preferred proof: campaign results, channel comparison data, time saved

### E-Commerce Manager content
- Lead with website performance: conversion rate, booking engine performance, mobile optimization
- Technical vocabulary acceptable: schema, structured data, A/B testing, personalization
- Show the technical capability without making it feel like an IT project
- Preferred proof: conversion rate uplifts, mobile vs. desktop data

### General Manager content
- Lead with business outcomes: revenue, occupancy, guest satisfaction, competitive position
- Minimal technical vocabulary: explain acronyms
- Frame technology as solving operational problems, not adding complexity
- Preferred proof: customer stories from similar hotels, overall ROI, market share

### Generic / all audience
- Use the broadest hospitality vocabulary
- Lead with industry trends and what's at stake for hotels broadly
- Balance commercial outcomes with strategic perspective
- Preferred format: thought leadership, industry analysis, trend pieces

## 15. Audience pain points (what hoteliers actually worry about)

Use these to anchor content to real problems, not invented ones:

1. Fragmented data, attribution, and reporting burden: conflicting numbers across systems, manual reporting, weak confidence in ROI
2. Integration complexity and brittle tech stacks: PMS, CRS, RMS, booking engines hard to connect; integrations break on updates
3. Limited visibility into the full guest journey: can't see end-to-end behavior from first touch to booking, blocks good channel/spend decisions
4. Over-dependence on OTAs and parity frustrations: feeling trapped, B2B and package tactics undermining parity
5. Booking engine UX and capability gaps: slow, poor mobile UX, limited testing and analytics
6. Weak foundations for personalization and audience intelligence: duplicate guest profiles, siloed data
7. Tool sprawl and fear of lock-in: too many point solutions, distrust of "AI/integration" promises
8. Non-room revenue hard to measure: F&B, spa, meetings not connected back to marketing
9. Organizational misalignment and skill gaps: marketing, revenue, ops use different metrics
10. C-suite expectation mismatch: leadership wants simple "AI" answers, teams need nuanced, trusted data

## 17. Red flags to fix

If any of these appear in a draft, revise before publishing:

- Starting with "In this article, we will..."
- Any sentence with an em dash
- "Leverage", "synergies", "seamless", "streamline", "holistic", "robust", "cutting-edge", "next-gen"
- Claims without supporting data or attribution
- Passive voice ("it has been found that", "hotels are advised to")
- Vague CTAs ("click here to learn more")
- More than one exclamation mark per piece
- Headlines in Title Case Instead Of Sentence Case
- Third person when addressing the reader directly ("hoteliers should consider" instead of "you should consider")
- Long introductory paragraphs before the first real point
- Features described without a "why it matters for your hotel" line
- Product pitch isolated from practical advice
- British English in newly generated content
